Income Inequality in Canada: The Past 50 Years

Introduction

This page is limited to discussing high-level data on income inequality in Canada. It does not address the causes or consequences of changes in inequality, and the only sub-national breakdown included is by province.

Income Inequality Measurement

There are several ways to measure income inequality. This page presents data using two of them.

The first is the Gini coefficient, which measures income inequality across an entire population. It ranges from 0 (perfect equality – everyone earns the same) to 1 (perfect inequality – one person earns all the income).

The second is income share, which measures the fraction of total income received by a particular group. These series focus on the share held by a specific segment of the distribution (e.g., the top 1%, top 10%, or bottom 50%). For example, if the top 1%'s share of market income is 15%, it means that the highest-earning 1% in terms of market income collectively received 15% of all market income. The shares for each income concept are computed separately, so those in the top 1% for market income are not necessarily those in the top 1% for total or after-tax income. Similarly, those in the top 1% in a given year are not necessarily those in the top 1% the following year.

Trends in Market Income Inequality

Market income is comprised of employment income (including both salaries and commissions), investment income, private retirement income, and other income earned in the market. It captures incomes prior to government redistribution and taxation.

The Gini coefficient for market income increased from 0.384 in 1976 to 0.436 in 2024. The increase mainly occurred from 1980 to 1995. Since 1995, the Gini coefficient has hovered between 0.421 and 0.446.

The top 1%’s share of market income increased from 7.6% in 1982 to 11.6% in 2023. The top 10%’s share increased from 32.2% in 1982 to 38.9% in 2023. The bottom 50%’s share decreased from 14.1% in 1982 to 10.8% in 2023. Over this period, the top 1% and top 10%’s shares peaked in 2006 and 2007, while the bottom 50%’s nadir was in 2020.

Taken together, these data series show that market income inequality in Canada has significantly increased over this period.

Trends in Total Income Inequality

Total income is comprised of market income and government transfers. Government transfers include retirement benefits such as OAS, GIS, CPP, and QPP, employment insurance, child benefits, social assistance, workers compensation, and GST/HST credits. It captures incomes after government benefits, but prior to taxation.

The Gini coefficient for total income increased from 0.330 in 1976 to 0.351 in 2024. The increase mainly occurred from 1976 to 2004. The Gini has since hovered around 0.350 – except in 2020, when it decreased to 0.322.

The top 1%’s share of market income increased from 7.1% in 1982 to 10.1% in 2023. The top 10%’s share increased from 30.2% in 1982 to 34.2% in 2023. The bottom 50%’s share has remained fairly stable, going from 17.8% in 1982 to 17.9% in 2023. Over this period, the top 1% and top 10%’s shares peaked in 2006 and 2007, while the bottom 50%’s nadir was during the early 2000s.

Taken together, these data series show that total income inequality in Canada has increased over this period – but not as much as market income inequality.

Trends in After-Tax Income Inequality

After-tax income is equal to total income minus income taxes. Since it captures incomes after government benefits and taxation, it is a measure of “disposable” income.

The Gini coefficient for after-tax income has remained fairly stable, going from 0.300 in 1976 to 0.305 in 2024. It increased from 1976 to 2004 – reaching 0.322 – before falling slightly over the past two decades.

The top 1%’s share of market income increased from 5.8% in 1982 to 7.7% in 2023. The top 10%’s share increased from 27.4% in 1982 to 30.1% in 2023. The bottom 50%’s share has remained fairly stable, going from 20.3% in 1982 to 20.3% in 2023. Over this period, the top 1% and top 10%’s shares peaked in 2006 and 2007, while the bottom 50%’s nadir was in 2007.

Taken together, these data series show that after-tax income inequality in Canada has increased slightly over this period – but not as much as total income inequality, and certainly much less than market income inequality.
