Wealth Inequality in Canada Since 2010

Introduction

Canadian households have grown wealthier since 2010, but that wealth remains heavily concentrated at the top. Using Statistics Canada's distributional wealth accounts, this page looks at how net worth is distributed across wealth quintiles, what that wealth is made up of, and how those patterns have shifted since 2010.

These data are based on household (not individual) wealth. Statistics Canada defines a household as a "person or group of persons who occupy the same dwelling and do not have a usual place of residence elsewhere in Canada or abroad." Households are ranked and grouped into quintiles by net worth, from the lowest quintile (least wealthy 20% of households) to the highest quintile (wealthiest 20% of households).

Two types of statistics are used. First, the wealth share gives the share of the national total held by a given quintile, expressed as a percentage. Second, the value per household gives the average dollar amount per household within a given quintile. There are, of course, other statistics that can be used to analyze wealth inequality, but these are the most convenient for these data.

This page is limited to discussing high-level data on wealth inequality in Canada. It does not address the causes or consequences of changes in inequality, and the only breakdowns included are by wealth quintile and asset and liability type.

Concentration of Net Worth

Wealth in Canada remains heavily concentrated at the top. As of Q4 of 2025, the highest wealth quintile holds 65.7% of all household net worth. The fourth quintile holds 21.5%, the third holds 9.8%, and the second holds 3.1%. The lowest quintile has essentially zero (or slightly negative) net worth, holding -0.1% of the total. These households have debts that, on average, equal or exceed their assets.

Net worth is equal to assets minus liabilities. Assets are similarly distributed to net worth, with the highest wealth quintile holding 60.3%. The fourth quintile holds 21.7%, the third holds 11.5%, the second holds 4.9%, and the lowest holds 1.6%. Liabilities are more evenly distributed. The fifth quintile holds 29.5%, the fourth holds 23.0%, the third holds 21.5%, the second holds 15.1%, and the lowest holds 10.9%. Therefore, wealthier households carry a disproportionately smaller share of debt relative to their share of assets, which drives inequality of net worth.

The average net worth of the highest quintile is $3,543,579, of the fourth is $1,160,145, of the third is $526,185, of the second is $167,907, and of the lowest is -$4,608.

Composition of Net Worth

The composition of net worth differs sharply by quintile. For the highest quintile, roughly one third of assets are non-financial (mainly real estate) and two thirds are financial (mainly those other than life insurance and pensions). Further down the wealth distribution, real estate's share of assets rises while the share held in financial assets other than life insurance and pensions falls.

Mortgage debt dominates liabilities for every quintile, but not evenly. It comprises the vast majority of total liabilities for the top four quintiles, but only half for the lowest quintile. This means that lowest quintile carries a much larger share of non-mortgage debt (such as credit cards, lines of credit, and vehicle loans).

Evolution of Net Worth

Average net worth has risen across every quintile since 2010. The top three quintiles have roughly doubled, the second quintile has roughly tripled, and the lowest quintile's average net worth has climbed from -$11,986 to -$4,608.

Each quintile's share of total wealth has increased, except for the highest quintile. For the highest quintile, its share of total assets decreased (driven by a decrease in its share of financial assets) and total liabilities increased (driven by an increase in its share of mortgage liabilities). For the fourth quintile, its share of total assets increased (driven by an increase in its share of both financial and non-financial assets) and total liabilities increased (driven by an increase in its share of mortgage liabilities). For the lowest quintile, its share of total assets remained the same and total liabilities decreased (driven by a decrease in its share of both mortgage and other liabilities).

The increase in average net worth across every quintile has not been steady. For all quintiles, average net worth peaked in Q1 of 2022, as Canada was emerging from the COVID-19 pandemic. This was primarily driven by a peak in the value of real estate. For the bottom four quintiles, their share of total wealth also peaked in Q1 of 2022, while the highest quintile's share hit its nadir.

Housing Wealth

Housing wealth in Canada has received significant policy and media attention in recent years. Real estate is more evenly distributed than net worth and other types of assets, though still unevenly distributed. The highest wealth quintile holds 51.0% of all real estate wealth. The fourth quintile holds 24.9%, the third holds 15.2%, and the second holds 6.6%, and the lowest holds 2.3%. For the bottom three quintiles, this is lower than the share in 2010, suggesting that the increase in housing wealth since then has mainly benefited the top of the distribution.

On average, the value of real estate is $1,310,122 for the highest quintile, $640,035 for the fourth, $391,488 for the third, $170,329 for the second, and $58,544 for the lowest. As previously mentioned, it comprises a higher share of net worth for less wealthy households. Furthermore, real estate was the main contributor to a peak in average net worth in Q1 of 2022.

The highest wealth quintile holds 30.4% of mortgage liabilities, the fourth holds 24.1%, the third holds 22.5%, the second holds 15.0%, and the lowest holds 8.0%. On average, this is $212,626 for the highest quintile, $168,573 for the fourth, $157,704 for the third, $104,948 for the second, and $55,686 for the lowest.
