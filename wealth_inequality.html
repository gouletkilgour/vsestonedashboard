Wealth Inequality in Canada: The Past 50 Years

Introduction

Wealth Inequality Measurement

There are several ways to measure wealth inequality, but this page presents data using one of them: wealth shares. A wealth share measures the fraction of all net personal wealth that is held by a particular group of individuals. These series focus on the wealth share of a particular segment of the distribution (i.e., top 1%, top 10%, bottom 50%). For example, if the top 1%’s share of net personal wealth is 15%, it means that the wealthiest 1% collectively hold 15% of all net personal wealth.

Net personal wealth is equal to non-financial assets, plus financial assets, minus liabilities. Non-financial assets are comprised of housing assets and business and other non-financial assets. Financial assets are comprised of currency, deposits, bonds, and loans, equities and fund shares, and pension funds and life insurance.

These series are based on individual, not household, wealth – but household wealth is assumed to be split equally between household members. The data come from the World Inequality Database.

Trends in Wealth Shares

The top 1%'s share of wealth climbed from 23.58% in 1980 to 28.58% in 2024, with nearly all of that gain concentrated in the three decades before 2010. Since then, the share has held fairly steady, though it peaked at 29.54% in 2021.

The top 10% saw a similar pattern: their share rose from 55.58% to 59.79% over the same period, driven mostly by growth before 2010. It plateaued afterward, with a 2021 peak of 60.65%.

The bottom 50% moved in the opposite direction, their share slipping from 15.48% to 13.98%. As with the other groups, the bulk of that change happened before 2010, after which things leveled off. It reached its nadir of 13.80% in 2021.
